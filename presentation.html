Part 3

The Workflow (The Brain Behind the Brain)

This is the part most people skip. And the part that matters most.

The Two Enforcement Tools

Superpowers

Makes Claude use your installed skills instead of ignoring them and freestyling. By default, Claude skips skills, rationalizes why it doesn't need them, and gives generic output. Superpowers makes skill invocation mandatory. It also forces brainstorming before planning. Think of it as the discipline layer.

OpenSpec

Spec-driven workflow. Creates actual artifacts at each stage: proposal, design, task list. Not optional. They're files that get created, reviewed, then executed. You can't skip from brainstorm to build because the build step literally requires the artifacts to exist first.

Together they create a 4-step sequence that is hard to shortcut.

1

Brainstorming + Research

Superpowers triggers brainstorming mode. Deep Search pulls data from multiple platforms. Structured Reasoning challenges assumptions. You explore the problem space. No commitments yet.

Deep Search + Structured Reasoning
2

Proposal

OpenSpec creates proposal.md. What are we building, why, for whom, what does success look like. A one-page document that forces you to articulate the idea before building it. Most bad AI output traces back to a vague brief. The proposal kills vagueness.

OpenSpec → proposal.md
3

Planning

OpenSpec creates design.md and tasks.md. The how and the implementation steps. This is where Structured Reasoning earns its keep. Tradeoff analysis, first principles, assumption testing on the actual design decisions.

OpenSpec → design.md + tasks.md
4

Implementation

Now build. The proposal gives you clarity. The design gives you architecture. The task list gives you sequence. Every skill and tool you need was identified in step 3. Now you just run them.

Execute with full context
Why this matters: Most people go straight from brainstorm to implementation. 3 hours later they have something that kind of works but doesn't quite fit and needs to be redone. The middle steps cost 15 minutes and a few hundred tokens. They save hours of rework and thousands of wasted tokens. Better input, better output. Every time.

What you need to get good at

Context Management

Claude is only as good as what it knows. The right context, not too much and not too little, is the single biggest leverage point.

Prompting

Not tricks and hacks. Real prompting: clear about what you want, giving constraints, providing examples, specifying output. Treat Claude like a brilliant new hire. Clear briefs, not mind-reading.

Strategic Thinking

The AI does the execution. You do the judgment. What to build, who it's for, what matters. This is the part that doesn't automate.

Why I use OpenClaw: I need multiple specialized agents working together. Not one generalist. A small team that works 24/7.

What a Good Agent Setup Looks Like

Identity

Agents know who they are, what they do, and how they communicate.

Memory

They remember last session. Decisions persist. Context carries forward.

Specialization

Each agent is good at one thing. Generalists produce generic output.

Coordination

Someone decides who does what. Without orchestration, agents step on each other.

Part 5

Build Your Own Skills

This is the most valuable section. My private skills took months to refine. But the process is learnable, and it changes how you use AI permanently.

What is a skill, really? A markdown file with triggers (when should it activate?), steps (the exact sequence), context (background knowledge), constraints (what it should NOT do), and output format (what "done" looks like). That's it. A structured brief that turns a vague request into a repeatable, high-quality process.

The Process

01

Identify the pattern

What task do you do repeatedly that requires the same thinking every time? If you've explained it to Claude more than twice, it's a skill candidate.

02

Write the manual version

Before automating, write out exactly how YOU do it. Step one, what info, what decisions, what good vs bad looks like. Hardest step. Most people can't articulate their own process.

03

Structure it as SKILL.md

Convert to triggers, steps, context, constraints, output. Be specific. "Write good copy" is not a step. "Write a headline that names the target audience's #1 pain point using their exact language" is.

04

Test and iterate

Run on a real task. First version: 60% right. The gap between what you meant and what Claude understood is where the learning happens. Three iterations gets you to 90%+.

The meta-skill: Building skills IS the skill. Once you can turn any repeated process into a reliable AI workflow, you're not dependent on anyone else's toolkit. You build exactly what you need when you need it. The three free skills are useful. The ability to build your own is worth 10x more.
